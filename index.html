Lexicographers have to keep updating the dictionary to accomodate new slang, subcultural idioms, and different expressions, in order to maintain a categorical and trustworthy order, like a building, lest it falls apart due to its axiomatic structures being under duress. Otherwise, "Official Communication Letter to The United States Coast Guard" and "Fried Bananas Are Crazy My Name's Philip" may start to mean the same thing. In mathematics, there are axioms. In philosophy, there are axioms. In programming, there are also axioms. Therefore, in HTML, the language of the web, it should be no different. Rules, specifications, and standards exist. We should not be scared, but rather stand in awe of the marvelous abstract structural monument that was built, is being fixed daily, and is trusted by millions to keep working indefinitely (uptime-wise and future-wise). And it is freely available for our consultation. Therefore, if we are to call ourselves HTML professionals, there is categorically no other option than to peruse the sacred tomes...

Below, you will find brief descriptions of these remarkable institutions that I have collected from around the Web.

The W3C is the main international standards organization for the World Wide Web. Founded in 1994 and led by Tim Berners-Lee, the consortium is made up of member organizations that maintain full-time staff working together in the development of standards for the World Wide Web. As of 21 March 2022, W3C had 459 members. W3C also engages in education and outreach, develops software and serves as an open forum for discussion about the Web. Source: W3C Wikipedia Article,
W3C HTML Standards

The WHATWG is a community that maintains and develops web standards, including DOM, Fetch and HTML. Employees of Apple, Mozilla, and Opera established WHATWG in 2004. Source: WHATWG MDN Article,
HTML Standards WHATWG: HTML Living Standard

HTML is easy to learn - you will enjoy it! We highly recommend Notepad. Source: W3Schools HTML Tutorial
